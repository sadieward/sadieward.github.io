Impact of Immigration

Public opinion on immigration, the process by which individuals become permanent residents or citizens of another country, is divided in the UK, with many misconceptions on the impact on GDP, unemployment and productivity. It’s also highly politized and was a major concern for the public voting in the Brexit Referendum, which resulted in greater restrictions for EU migrants, but had no effect on non-EU migrants. This project sets out to analyse the impact of immigration using real-world data.

This chart shows the immigrants coming into the UK are more likely to be young, working age individuals, with most of the distribution under the age of 45. This suggests immigration improves the age dependency ratio and has positive fiscal effects on the economy

Chart 4 - Unemployment

A major concern of the public is migrants arriving in the UK with little willingness to work, however this chart shows the unemployment for the UK-born and EU-born are very similar. It is slightly higher for non-EU born migrants; however, they have converged slightly over the last 2 decades, with the unemployment rate for non-EU born migrants only being 2 percentage points higher in 2022.

Chart 5 - GDP


Github Colab

This chart shows that, as expected, countries with a higher number of immigrants have a larger economy, and therefore greater GDP. This is consistent with the theory that immigration increases the labour supply and demand in the economy, stimulating growth.

Chart 6 - GDP per capita

This shows a positive correlation between GDP per capita and immigration, with an R squared of 0.64. This does not, however, imply a causal relationship.

Chart 7 - Productivity

Data

Most of the data was obtained from the ONS and the OECD, and downloaded as csv files. For the first chart, I would have liked to batch download it as an API, however this was only available through FRED, which had only had data for net migration every 5 years up to 2015. I instead downloaded the data as a csv file.


For chart 4, I used an API from the ONS, which will therefore automatically update when new data is released.


As the project focuses on the UK, I wanted to analyse how productivity within UK industries is impacted by the foreign-born workforce in that industry, however I could only find data for this for 6 industries on the ONS, which would not be enough points to establish an accurate correlation. Instead, I chose to compare OECD countries, as seen in chart 5 and 6. I manually took out countries that were outliers, such as Luxembourg. In chart 5, I scraped data from the statisticstimes to match the countries with their respective continents. This was done in Colab.


Data Cleaning

For charts 3, 5 and 6 I merged datasets using in python using Colab, which avoided any mismatch errors. Colab was also used to convert wide format 3-dimensional data into long format for chart 1.


The were issues with the data for chart 3 as the data for the age distribution for the general population went up in increments of 4 up to 90, however the age distribution for immigrants only went up in increments of 15 for the later years. I fixed this through performing calculations in excel to allow the datasets to match up.


The API data for chart 4 needed to be cleaned within vega-lite, which I found challenging as I don’t have much experience with this, however I eventually got it to work.

Conclusion

To conclude, immigration appears to have overall positive effects on the economy as the influx of young working age individuals reduces the age dependency ratio, having positive fiscal effects. It is also associated with higher levels of GDP and GDP per capita, however this is not necessarily a causal relationship. As no correlation was found for productivity and immigration, it may be insightful to analyse industry data.
